What Is Chapter 7 Bankruptcy?

A free, plain-language guide to the most common type of bankruptcy -- the liquidation chapter. No ads, no lead generation, no strings attached.

What Is Chapter 7?

Chapter 7 is the "liquidation" chapter of the Bankruptcy Code (11 U.S.C. sections 701--784). It is the most common type of consumer bankruptcy in the United States, accounting for roughly 60% of all bankruptcy filings each year.

In a Chapter 7 case, a court-appointed trustee reviews the debtor's assets, sells any property that is not protected by exemptions, and distributes the proceeds to creditors. In exchange, the debtor receives a discharge -- a court order that permanently eliminates most unsecured debts.

The entire process typically takes about 4 to 6 months from filing to discharge. In practice, most Chapter 7 cases are "no-asset" cases, meaning the debtor's property is fully exempt and the trustee has nothing to sell. The debtor keeps everything and walks away debt-free.

Who Qualifies for Chapter 7?

Not everyone can file Chapter 7. Congress added the "means test" in 2005 (11 U.S.C. section 707(b)) to ensure that debtors with the ability to repay some portion of their debts use Chapter 13 instead. Here is how qualification works:

  • Income below state median: If your household income is below the median for your state and household size, you automatically pass the means test. No further calculation is needed.
  • Income above state median: You must complete the full means test, which deducts allowed expenses from your income. If the remaining "disposable income" is below a threshold, you still qualify. Visit meanstest.org to learn more about how the means test works.
  • Prior filing restrictions: You cannot receive a Chapter 7 discharge if you received a prior Chapter 7 discharge within 8 years (section 727(a)(8)) or a Chapter 13 discharge within 6 years (section 727(a)(9)). Check your eligibility at 727a8.com.
  • Credit counseling: You must complete a credit counseling course from an approved provider within 180 days before filing.

What Debts Are Discharged?

The Chapter 7 discharge eliminates most unsecured debts, but some debts survive bankruptcy by law. Here is the breakdown:

Debts typically discharged:

  • Credit card balances
  • Medical bills
  • Personal loans and payday loans
  • Past-due utility bills
  • Deficiency balances after repossession or foreclosure
  • Most lawsuit judgments

Debts that usually survive (nondischargeable):

  • Student loans (unless you prove "undue hardship" in an adversary proceeding -- rare but possible)
  • Recent income taxes (generally taxes due within the last 3 years)
  • Child support and alimony (domestic support obligations)
  • Debts obtained through fraud, false pretenses, or false financial statements
  • Fines and penalties owed to government agencies
  • Debts from DUI/DWI injuries

For a complete list of nondischargeable debts under 11 U.S.C. section 523(a), visit 523a.org and nondischargeable.org.

What Property Do You Keep?

Bankruptcy exemptions determine what property you keep. Exempt property cannot be taken by the trustee or sold to pay creditors. The specific exemptions available depend on your state.

  • Federal vs. state exemptions: Some states let you choose between federal bankruptcy exemptions (11 U.S.C. section 522(d)) and state exemptions. Other states require you to use only state exemptions. You cannot mix and match.
  • Homestead exemption: Protects equity in your primary residence. Amounts vary dramatically -- from a few thousand dollars in some states to unlimited protection in Texas and Florida.
  • Vehicle exemption: Protects equity in your car. The federal exemption is approximately $4,450 (adjusted periodically). Many states offer more.
  • Retirement accounts: 401(k), IRA, pension, and similar qualified retirement accounts are almost always fully exempt. IRAs are protected up to approximately $1.5 million.
  • Personal property: Household goods, clothing, appliances, tools of the trade, and similar items are typically exempt up to specified dollar amounts.
  • Wildcard exemption: Some states (and the federal system) offer a "wildcard" exemption that can be applied to any property, which is especially useful for protecting cash or other assets that do not fit neatly into another category.

In most consumer Chapter 7 cases, all of the debtor's property is exempt. The trustee files a "no distribution" report and the debtor keeps everything.

The Chapter 7 Process

Here is how a typical Chapter 7 case unfolds, from start to finish:

  1. Credit counseling (pre-filing): Complete a credit counseling course from a U.S. Trustee-approved provider. This must be done within 180 days before filing. The course typically takes about an hour and costs $15 to $50.
  2. File the petition: The debtor files a voluntary petition along with schedules listing all assets, debts, income, expenses, and recent financial transactions. The filing fee is $338. The automatic stay takes effect immediately, stopping most creditor actions.
  3. 341 meeting of creditors: Held 20 to 40 days after filing. The trustee asks questions under oath about the debtor's finances. Creditors may attend but rarely do. The meeting usually lasts 5 to 10 minutes. Learn more at 341meeting.org.
  4. Trustee review: The Chapter 7 trustee reviews the debtor's assets to determine whether any nonexempt property exists. In "no-asset" cases (the vast majority), the trustee files a report stating there is nothing to distribute.
  5. Debtor education course (post-filing): Before receiving a discharge, the debtor must complete a financial management course (sometimes called "debtor education"). This is a separate requirement from the pre-filing credit counseling.
  6. Discharge: If no one objects, the court enters a discharge order roughly 60 to 90 days after the 341 meeting. This permanently eliminates qualifying debts.
  7. Case closed: After the trustee completes all administration (or files a no-distribution report), the case is closed. Total timeline: about 4 to 6 months.

Chapter 7 vs. Chapter 13

The two most common types of consumer bankruptcy serve different purposes. Here is a quick comparison:

Feature Chapter 7 Chapter 13
Type Liquidation Repayment plan
Duration 4 to 6 months 3 to 5 years
Means test Required Not required (income limits apply)
Property Nonexempt assets may be sold Keep all property
Mortgage arrears Cannot cure -- must be current Can cure arrears through plan
Car loan cramdown No (can redeem at value) Yes (if loan > 910 days old)
Income requirement Below median or pass means test Regular income required
Credit report impact Stays 10 years Stays 7 years
Refiling restriction 8 years (Ch. 7 to Ch. 7) 2 years (Ch. 13 to Ch. 13)

Neither chapter is universally "better." The right choice depends on your income, assets, debts, and goals. If you are behind on your mortgage and want to keep your home, Chapter 13 may be the better option. If you have mostly unsecured debt and want a quick fresh start, Chapter 7 is usually faster and simpler.

Common Questions

How long does Chapter 7 take?

Most Chapter 7 cases take about 4 to 6 months from filing to discharge. The 341 meeting of creditors is usually held 20 to 40 days after filing. If no one objects, the discharge order is entered roughly 60 to 90 days after the 341 meeting.

Does Chapter 7 affect my credit?

Yes. A Chapter 7 bankruptcy stays on your credit report for 10 years from the filing date. However, many filers see their credit scores begin to recover within 1 to 2 years because the discharge eliminates the underlying debt. The fresh start often improves your debt-to-income ratio immediately.

Can I keep my car in Chapter 7?

Usually yes. If your equity in the vehicle is within your state's exemption limits, you keep it. If you have a car loan, you can typically reaffirm the debt (agree to keep paying) and keep the vehicle. Some states also allow you to redeem the vehicle by paying its current fair market value in a lump sum.

Can I keep my house in Chapter 7?

It depends on your equity and your state's homestead exemption. If your equity is within the exemption limit, the trustee cannot sell the home. You must stay current on mortgage payments -- Chapter 7 does not provide a mechanism to catch up on missed payments the way Chapter 13 does.

How much does Chapter 7 cost?

The court filing fee is $338. Attorney fees vary by region but typically range from $1,000 to $2,500. The two required courses (credit counseling and debtor education) cost about $15 to $50 each. If you cannot afford the filing fee, you can apply to pay in installments or request a fee waiver if your income is below 150% of the poverty line.

Will I lose my tax refund?

Possibly. A tax refund that you are owed on the filing date is property of the bankruptcy estate. The trustee may claim all or part of it. Timing your filing carefully -- for example, filing after you have received and spent your refund on necessary expenses -- can help. Discuss timing strategy with a bankruptcy attorney.
